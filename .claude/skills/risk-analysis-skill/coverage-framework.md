## 1. Property & Business Interruption

### Core Exposures Addressed
Physical damage to owned/leased buildings, contents, equipment, stock/inventory, and
improvements & betterments. Business income loss and extra expense resulting from covered
physical damage. Contingent business income from damage to key suppliers or customers.

### Key Coverage Components
- Building and contents at replacement cost vs. actual cash value
- Business income with agreed value (eliminates coinsurance disputes)
- Extra expense — costs to maintain operations during restoration
- Contingent business income / contingent extra expense
- Ordinance or law coverage (demolition, increased cost of construction, loss to
  undamaged portion)
- Equipment breakdown (boiler & machinery) — critical for buildings with HVAC, electrical,
  and mechanical systems
- Flood (standalone NFIP or excess/private market)
- Earthquake / earth movement
- Named windstorm (especially coastal)
- Transit / inland marine for goods in motion
- Fine arts, valuable papers, accounts receivable
- Debris removal, pollutant cleanup
- Utility services — direct damage and time element

### Key Structural Decisions
- Blanket vs. scheduled values
- Occurrence vs. annual aggregate limits for CAT perils
- Waiting period / deductible structure for time element (hours vs. days)
- Coinsurance vs. agreed amount
- Valuation basis (replacement cost, functional replacement, ACV)
- Vacancy provisions and their impact on coverage
- Ordinance or law sublimits relative to actual exposure

### Common Gaps to Check
- Flood sublimit inadequacy in high-hazard zones
- Equipment breakdown excluded or sublimited
- Business income period of restoration too short
- CBI triggers too narrow (requiring physical damage to named supplier)
- Ordinance or law excluded or minimal sublimit
- Service interruption (utility failure) gaps
- Debris removal sublimit inadequacy after major loss

## 2. General Liability (CGL)

### Core Exposures Addressed
Third-party bodily injury and property damage arising from premises, operations, products,
and completed operations. Personal and advertising injury. Medical payments. Contractual
liability (insured contracts).

### Key Coverage Components
- Premises/operations liability
- Products/completed operations liability (critical for manufacturers, contractors, food)
- Personal and advertising injury
- Contractual liability — defense and indemnity for assumed liabilities
- Medical payments (goodwill coverage, no-fault)
- Fire legal liability (damage to rented premises)

### Key Structural Decisions
- Occurrence vs. claims-made trigger (occurrence is standard for GL)
- Per-occurrence and general aggregate limits
- Products/completed operations aggregate (separate from general aggregate)
- Additional insured endorsements — ongoing operations vs. completed operations
- Primary and noncontributory endorsement
- Waiver of subrogation

### Common Gaps to Check
- Contractual liability exclusion modifications (watch for manuscript forms)
- Classification code accuracy (impacts premium and coverage scope)
- Designated premises limitation (multi-location operations)
- Independent contractors exclusion or limitation
- Pollution exclusion scope (total vs. hostile fire, sudden & accidental)
- Employment-related practices exclusion
- Professional services exclusion (carved back or not)
- Damage to your product / damage to your work exclusions

## 3. Commercial Auto

### Core Exposures Addressed
Liability for bodily injury and property damage arising from owned, hired, and non-owned
vehicles. Physical damage to owned fleet. Uninsured/underinsured motorist exposure.

### Key Coverage Components
- Auto liability (owned, hired, non-owned)
- Auto physical damage (comprehensive and collision)
- Uninsured/underinsured motorist
- Medical payments / PIP (state-dependent)
- Hired auto physical damage
- Hired auto liability (employees using rental vehicles)
- Non-owned auto liability (employees using personal vehicles for work)
- Motor carrier (if applicable — MCS-90, BMC-91)
- Garagekeepers (if applicable)

### Key Structural Decisions
- Combined single limit vs. split limits
- Symbol selection (1 = any auto, 2 = owned only, 8 = hired, 9 = non-owned)
- Fleet vs. non-fleet rating
- Radius of operations
- Telematics / usage-based insurance integration
- Deductible structure for physical damage

### Common Gaps to Check
- Non-owned auto coverage gap (employees using personal vehicles)
- Hired auto gap (especially short-term rentals)
- Trailer interchange coverage
- Loading/unloading coverage alignment with GL
- Long-haul / interstate filing requirements
- MCS-90 endorsement need for motor carriers
- Pollution liability arising from auto (chemical transport)

## 4. Workers' Compensation & Employers Liability

### Core Exposures Addressed
Statutory workers' compensation benefits for work-related injury or illness. Employers
liability for claims outside the WC exclusive remedy (dual capacity, third-party over,
consequential BI). Stop-gap coverage in monopolistic states.

### Key Coverage Components
- Part A: Workers' Compensation (statutory — no limit)
- Part B: Employers Liability (typically $1M/$1M/$1M)
- USL&H (United States Longshore & Harbor Workers Act)
- Jones Act / maritime coverage
- Federal workers' comp endorsements (FELA, etc.)
- Voluntary compensation
- Foreign voluntary workers' comp
- Stop-gap employers liability (OH, ND, WA, WY)

### Key Structural Decisions
- Guaranteed cost vs. loss-sensitive program (retro, large deductible, captive)
- Experience modification factor management
- State-by-state filing requirements and monopolistic state handling
- OCIP/CCIP enrollment for construction operations
- Return-to-work program integration
- Wrap-up vs. practice programs for project-based work

### Common Gaps to Check
- All-states endorsement (operations in states not listed)
- Monopolistic state stop-gap coverage
- USL&H / maritime exposure not endorsed
- Alternate employer endorsement (staffing/temp labor)
- Voluntary comp for excluded employments
- Foreign voluntary WC for traveling employees
- Leased worker / PEO coverage clarity

## 5. Umbrella / Excess Liability

### Core Exposures Addressed
Catastrophic liability beyond primary limits. Umbrella provides broader coverage (drop-down)
plus higher limits; excess follows form of underlying.

### Key Coverage Components
- Excess coverage over scheduled underlying (GL, Auto, EL)
- Drop-down coverage for claims not covered by underlying (umbrella only)
- Defense obligation (duty to defend vs. indemnity only)
- Self-insured retention for drop-down coverage

### Key Structural Decisions
- Umbrella (broader) vs. following-form excess (follows underlying terms)
- Lead umbrella vs. excess buffer layers
- Total tower height relative to exposure modeling
- Defense inside vs. outside the limits
- Retained limit / SIR for drop-down
- Scheduling of underlying policies (all underlying must be listed)

### Common Gaps to Check
- Underlying policy limits don't meet umbrella scheduled minimum
- Umbrella exclusions broader than underlying (especially pollution, professional, cyber)
- Auto liability not scheduled as underlying
- Employers liability not scheduled
- Defense costs eroding limits (inside limits)
- Follow-form excess not actually following all underlying terms
- Gap between underlying aggregate exhaustion and excess attachment

## 6. Management Liability

### Directors & Officers (D&O)
- Side A: Direct coverage for individual directors/officers when company can't indemnify
- Side B: Reimburses company for indemnifying D&O
- Side C: Entity coverage for securities claims (public companies)
- Key concerns: securities litigation, regulatory investigations, breach of fiduciary duty,
  mismanagement, insolvency, bankruptcy carve-outs
- Structure: claims-made, retention applies to Side B/C, Side A typically no retention

### Employment Practices Liability (EPL)
- Wrongful termination, discrimination, harassment, retaliation, failure to promote
- Wage & hour coverage (increasingly available but often sublimited)
- Third-party coverage (harassment/discrimination by employees against non-employees)
- Structure: claims-made, per-claim and aggregate, self-insured retention

### Fiduciary Liability
- ERISA fiduciary duty claims related to benefit plan management
- Investment performance claims, fee litigation
- Plan administration errors
- Voluntary correction program costs
- Structure: claims-made, protects plan fiduciaries

### Crime / Fidelity (sometimes packaged here)
- Employee theft, forgery, computer fraud, social engineering fraud
- Funds transfer fraud, impersonation fraud
- Third-party crime coverage
- ERISA fidelity bond (required for plans with participant-directed accounts)

### Common Gaps to Check
- D&O Side A adequacy (is there dedicated Side A DIC?)
- Insured vs. insured exclusion scope
- Wage & hour exclusion on EPL
- Prior acts / continuity date gaps on claims-made
- Cyber coverage overlap/gap with standalone cyber policy
- Antitrust exclusion on D&O
- Fiduciary coverage for health and welfare plans (not just retirement)

## 7. Cyber Liability

### Core Exposures Addressed
Data breach response costs, network security liability, privacy liability, media liability,
business interruption from cyber events, cyber extortion, regulatory defense and penalties,
PCI-DSS fines and assessments, social engineering / funds transfer fraud.

### Key Coverage Components
- First-party: breach response (forensics, notification, credit monitoring, PR), business
  interruption and extra expense from network outage, data restoration, cyber extortion
  / ransomware, contingent BI (vendor/cloud provider outages)
- Third-party: network security liability, privacy liability, regulatory defense and
  penalties, media/content liability, PCI fines and assessments

### Key Structural Decisions
- Standalone cyber vs. endorsement on package
- Waiting period for BI coverage (typically 8-12 hours)
- Sublimits vs. full policy limits for key coverages
- Retroactive date / prior acts
- Panel vs. choice of breach counsel and forensics vendors
- War / nation-state exclusion scope (critical post-2022)
- Systemic risk / infrastructure exclusion scope

### Common Gaps to Check
- Social engineering / funds transfer fraud sublimit or exclusion
- Contingent BI from cloud provider outage (AWS, Azure, GCP)
- War exclusion carve-back for non-state-attributed attacks
- Dependent business interruption triggers
- Voluntary vs. involuntary shutdown coverage
- Bodily injury arising from cyber event (IoT, OT/ICS)
- Full policy limit vs. sublimited breach response
- Overlap with crime policy on social engineering

## 8. Professional Liability / E&O

### Core Exposures Addressed
Claims arising from negligent acts, errors, or omissions in professional services rendered.
Failure to perform, misrepresentation, breach of duty in a professional capacity.

### Key Coverage Components
- Defense and indemnity for professional negligence claims
- Typically claims-made with retroactive date
- Covers both acts and omissions in rendering or failing to render services
- Technology E&O, design professional E&O, miscellaneous professional E&O, real estate E&O

### Key Structural Decisions
- Claims-made trigger — retroactive date is critical
- Prior acts / full prior acts coverage
- Per-claim and aggregate limit structure
- Defense inside vs. outside limits
- Named insured scope (entity, subsidiaries, individual professionals)
- Consent to settle clause (hammer clause)

### Common Gaps to Check
- Retroactive date gap (prior acts not fully covered)
- Bodily injury / property damage exclusion (may be needed for some professionals)
- Contractual liability exclusion scope
- Subcontractor/subconsultant E&O adequacy
- Network security / cyber acts covered or excluded
- Regulatory proceeding coverage
- Insured capacity definition (what constitutes "professional services")

## 9. Environmental / Pollution Liability

### Core Exposures Addressed
Pollution conditions on, at, under, or migrating from owned/operated sites. Third-party
claims for bodily injury or property damage from pollution. Cleanup costs. Transportation
pollution. Contractor's pollution.

### Key Coverage Components
- Site-specific pollution liability (known sites, scheduled)
- Contractor's pollution liability (for contractors performing work)
- Environmental professional E&O
- Transportation pollution
- Non-owned disposal site liability
- First-party cleanup coverage
- Natural resource damages
- Indoor environmental (mold, legionella, IAQ)

### Key Structural Decisions
- Claims-made vs. occurrence trigger
- Known vs. unknown conditions
- Scheduled sites vs. blanket coverage
- Regulatory defense and compliance costs
- Voluntary vs. mandated cleanup coverage
- Mold/legionella sublimits or exclusions

### Common Gaps to Check
- Total pollution exclusion on GL leaves no pollution coverage without standalone
- Transportation pollution gap (auto policy excludes, pollution policy may not cover)
- Non-owned disposal site coverage adequacy
- Indoor air quality (mold, legionella) coverage
- Historic contamination / pre-existing conditions
- PFAS / emerging contaminant coverage

## 10. Builders Risk / Course of Construction

### Core Exposures Addressed
Physical damage to structures under construction, renovation, or installation. Materials
in transit and in storage. Soft costs during construction delay.

### Key Coverage Components
- Completed value form (covers full project value from day one)
- Materials in transit, at temporary storage, at fabrication sites
- Soft costs (interest, taxes, insurance, loan fees during delay)
- Delay in completion / loss of rents
- Testing and commissioning coverage
- LEG (London Engineering Group) defect exclusion selection
  - LEG 1 (excludes all defect-related damage — most restrictive)
  - LEG 2 (covers resultant damage from defects — most common)
  - LEG 3 (covers cost to repair defect and resultant damage — broadest)
- Earthquake, flood, windstorm sub-limits

### Key Structural Decisions
- Project-specific vs. master builders risk (rolling program)
- Who procures — owner or contractor? (Impacts OCIP/CCIP design)
- Completed value vs. reporting form
- LEG exclusion selection
- Soft cost coverage period
- Named storm / CAT deductible structure
- Existing structures coverage for renovation projects
- Installation floater integration

### Common Gaps to Check
- Soft cost coverage period shorter than realistic delay scenario
- LEG exclusion version mismatch with contract requirements
- Existing structure damage during renovation
- Testing and commissioning gap
- Subcontractor default / design-build defect
- Earthquake/flood sublimit inadequacy
- Transition from builders risk to permanent property policy

- License and permit bonds
- Supply bonds
- Maintenance / warranty bonds
